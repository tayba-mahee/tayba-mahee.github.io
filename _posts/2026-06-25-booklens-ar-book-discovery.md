## Overview

Browsing a bookstore is an enjoyable experience, yet deciding which book to purchase often requires readers to leave that experience behind. Many readers pull out their phones to search Goodreads, compare Amazon reviews, or look up summaries before making a decision. This constant context switching interrupts the natural flow of exploring a bookstore.

**BookLens** explores how Augmented Reality can bridge the physical and digital worlds by providing contextual book information directly within the browsing experience. Instead of replacing bookstores, BookLens enhances them by helping readers make faster and more confident purchasing decisions.

## Project Details

**Role:** UX Researcher · Product Designer  
**Duration:** Human-Computer Interaction Course Project  
**Methods:** Product Discovery, UX Research, Competitive Analysis, User Interviews, Personas, Empathy Mapping, Task Flows, Usability Testing

## The Challenge

> How might we help readers make informed purchasing decisions without interrupting the experience of browsing a physical bookstore?

Current solutions require users to:
- Search multiple applications
- Read lengthy online reviews
- Compare prices manually
- Interrupt the in-store experience

The opportunity was to provide relevant information at the exact moment users need it.

## Research

### Secondary Research

To better understand the problem space, I reviewed literature and existing technologies related to:
- Book purchasing behavior
- Physical bookstore experiences
- Reading recommendation systems
- Augmented Reality applications
- Mobile commerce
- Consumer decision making

This research helped identify limitations in existing solutions and informed the product direction.

### Competitive Analysis

I analyzed several existing products, including:
- **Goodreads**
- **Amazon Books**
- **BookBuddy**
- **Blippar**

Although these platforms provide ratings, reviews, and recommendations, none integrate contextual information into the physical bookstore experience through augmented reality.

This revealed an opportunity to reduce friction by combining digital information with in-store browsing.

### User Interviews

To validate assumptions, I conducted informal interviews with readers to understand:
- How they discover books
- What information influences purchasing decisions
- Current browsing behaviors
- Frustrations during bookstore visits

#### Key Findings

- Readers frequently search Goodreads before purchasing unfamiliar books
- Comparing multiple books is time-consuming
- Users enjoy exploring bookstores but dislike switching between physical browsing and digital research
- Trustworthy recommendations significantly influence purchasing decisions

![Research Findings](/assets/img/booklens/05-research-findings.svg)

> How might we help readers access trusted information while preserving the enjoyable experience of browsing a bookstore?

## Concept Solution

**BookLens transforms a smartphone into an intelligent bookstore companion.**

Using the device's camera, users scan a book cover to instantly receive contextual information through augmented reality.

### Information Provided

- Book title
- Author
- Genre
- Goodreads rating
- Book summary
- Publication details
- Similar recommendations
- Online pricing
- User reviews

Rather than navigating multiple applications, readers receive relevant information directly within the browsing experience.

![AR Interface Wireframes](/assets/img/booklens/03-ar-interface-wireframes.svg)

## User Journey

### Existing Experience

**Browse shelf → Find interesting book → Search Goodreads → Search Amazon → Read reviews → Compare books → Return to shelf → Purchase**

**Pain Points:**
- Interrupted browsing
- Slow decision making
- Multiple applications
- Information overload

### Proposed Experience

**Browse shelf → Scan book → View AR information → Read summary → View ratings → Compare recommendations → Purchase confidently**

![User Journey Comparison](/assets/img/booklens/01-user-journey-comparison.svg)
_Comparison of current vs. proposed user experience showing reduced friction_

## Information Architecture

The experience was intentionally designed around a simple workflow:

**Home → Scanner → Book Details → Reviews → Recommendations → Purchase / Save**

The information hierarchy prioritizes essential information first while allowing users to explore additional details when needed.

![Information Architecture](/assets/img/booklens/02-information-architecture.svg)
_App structure showing navigation flow and content hierarchy_

## Design Principles

Several UX principles guided the concept:

### Reduce Cognitive Load
Present only the information users need at the moment they need it.

### Context Awareness
Integrate information into the physical environment rather than forcing users to leave the browsing experience.

### Progressive Disclosure
Display high-level information first while allowing users to explore additional details if desired.

### Trust
Surface information from familiar sources to increase confidence in purchasing decisions.

## Evaluation

Although the project timeline limited formal usability testing, informal evaluations were conducted to assess:
- Ease of learning
- Interface clarity
- Navigation
- Usefulness of augmented information
- Overall user experience

Feedback was incorporated into subsequent design iterations and feature prioritization.

## Reflection

This project fundamentally changed how I think about product design.

Rather than beginning with interface screens, I learned to begin with **user behavior**. By observing how readers naturally browse bookstores and validating assumptions through research, I was able to identify opportunities where augmented reality could meaningfully reduce friction without disrupting the experience itself.

The project also reinforced that **emerging technologies should never be introduced simply because they are innovative**. Their value comes from solving genuine user problems while fitting naturally into existing behaviors.

### Future Directions

If I were to continue this project, I would:
- Conduct field studies inside bookstores
- Explore computer vision models for automatic book recognition
- Validate the concept through higher-fidelity prototypes
- Conduct longitudinal usability testing
